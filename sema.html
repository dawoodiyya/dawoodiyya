Chapter One: The Garden

I am fortunate to live in a part of the city that shelters a beautiful park. The park is a garden, strangely secluded in a swirl of traffic. I spend some time walking there on most days since its main gate is hardly forty steps from my own door. The name of the garden, Namaz, is set in a bronze plaque in a stone and masonry wall at the gate, which opens onto a place of remarkable peace. I have often wondered how, in a city of this size, so few people have discovered its winding, peaceful paths.
Generally I am alone in the heavy scent of herbs seduced by the heat of the sun. Even the crisp morning air in the place is fragrant. Occasionally lovers can be seen at a distance towards evening. But mostly I am alone. The privacy of the garden is a wonder for another reason. It seems to have been caught on a corner of time, so that the cycles of years pass it by. The winter ice at the gate is overcast by a blossoming bough and fruits found in no other place are always in season. Yet so few people stop to enjoy them, that the ground under the trees is thick with the pungent odors of ripeness and rotting. There is indeed a gardener with whom I have on rare occasions stopped to chat, but I usually pass him with a nod and the briefest of greetings. He is often so rapt in his work, which he seems to do more for the enjoyment of it than for his salary, that he hardly seems to see me as I pass.
He surprised me this afternoon as I was engrossed in sampling one of the fruits that was just coming on. It is one I look forward to throughout the year, as it is available for only a few short days. I do not know its name, but have heard it to be of the genus Ramadhan. It is slightly smaller than a plum, of a translucent golden color with a blush of crimson. It is still quite hard when ripe, but very tart to the tongue. The unusual thing about it is the delightful smell of vanilla from the skin. After having eaten one, the smell stays on one's fingers for hours and no washing seems to diminish it.
As I was enjoying one of these delicacies and already eying another, I was startled at the approach of the gardener. Now all of the fruits in the garden are open to the public to enjoy merely on condition that none are removed from the premises. But the seclusion of the place inspires a sense of intruding on private property, and the property of a wealthy and powerful personage at that. So I looked up guiltily from my pleasurable task. Overlooking my embarrassment, the gardener paused to comment on the condition of the bushes. Then he abruptly turned to me and asked, "Have you ever visited the fountain on the eastern slope of the garden?" I was astonished. I had imagined that with all of my years of frequenting the place, I had discovered all there was to know about it. Suddenly I realized that, although the garden did not seem large, it held secrets of which I had been completely unaware. I had in fact spent years wandering over the same paths by force of habit, and all of my intimate knowledge of the garden was little more than mere illusion. At once a new boldness took hold on me and I answered, "No, sir, I did not even realize that the fountain existed. Would you be so kind as to lead me there?" "With pleasure," he said, and with that led me down the pathway.
We had not walked more than a few minutes when we came out of the shade of the trees into a bright glade. Against the hillside there was a sun-engulfed fountain that seemed to be bubbling out liquid diamonds. I stopped short and stared at the scene, amazed that such a place existed at my very fingertips for so long without my knowing of it. The gardener turned to go. "I hope you enjoy it," he said and then retraced his steps back to his labors.
I had already been admiring the beauties of the fountain for several minutes before I noticed a man sitting on the grass behind it. He wore a dark beard and was fingering a string of white and rare green onyx beads. The glint of the sun on his beads was what had attracted my attention. When the man noticed that I had seen him, he got up, produced a green onyx bowl, filled it with the clear liquid of the well, and brought it to me, saying at the same time, "Peace to you."
"And to you also peace," I replied. When I had tasted the drink I was amazed. For it was sweeter than honey, more refreshing than nectar of limes, and in some way more intoxicating than the finest wine. Yet the draught seemed to make my mind and senses clearer, so that the shadows about me disappeared and every leaf stood out in bold relief like so many living emeralds. "What is this well and why have I not found it sooner?" I asked the man. This is an ancient well, dug by the early prophets. Beside it sat one day the great prophet and Messiah, peace be upon him. He was very thirsty, and at that time the well was deep. A woman came to draw water and he asked her for a drink. Since that time the well has been full and running over, a fountain of living water that if anyone drinks of it, he will never again be thirsty. But it is true that few find it. If they search at all, they search too far away, not realizing that the fountain of living water is within the garden."
"And who are you, sir?" I asked somewhat discourteously I fear. I had the feeling that he not only knew who I was but the mere suggestion there might be anything he did not know was foolish. "I am the gate into the city," he said and took a golden key on a silver chain and opened a door in the hillside. As soon as he had opened the door and was standing beside it, it seemed to me that he turned into a great lion with a shining mane. When I saw the lion in front of the door my legs refused to support me and I fell to the ground fainting. Everything turned dark.
When I looked about me again, there was neither lion nor man, but only the fountain, the onyx beads on the grass, and before me an open door and a golden staircase.
